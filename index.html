Sengoku Stories

Nobunaga Oda and the Singing Bird

One of the most well-known legends about Oda Nobunaga comes from a proverb comparing him to other great warlords. Faced with a bird that wouldn’t sing, Nobunaga said, “Kill it.” This simple line symbolized his ruthless and decisive nature. Though apocryphal, the story reflects his reputation as a bold reformer who would stop at nothing to unify Japan, breaking traditions and crushing resistance to achieve his vision.

Toyotomi Hideyoshi and the One Thousand Tiles

Before becoming a great leader, Toyotomi Hideyoshi was known for his determination and cleverness. One legend tells of a time when he was tasked with tiling a large temple roof—a job that seemed impossible for a man of his rank and experience. Instead of complaining, he rallied local workers, organized them efficiently, and completed the task faster than anyone expected. His ability to lead and inspire, even in small tasks, caught the attention of Oda Nobunaga. This tale highlights how Hideyoshi’s brilliance in organization and people skills paved his way to becoming one of Japan’s most powerful figures.

Takeda Shingen and the Wind, Forest, Fire, Mountain

Takeda Shingen, the “Tiger of Kai,” was famous for his military tactics and his emblematic battle standard, which read: “Swift as the wind, silent as the forest, fierce as fire, and immovable as a mountain.” This motto, inspired by Sun Tzu's Art of War, became legendary and symbolized his strategic mastery on the battlefield. His ability to strike quickly and vanish or stand firm when needed earned him a fearsome reputation.

Uesugi Kenshin and the Gift of Salt

Uesugi Kenshin, known as the “Dragon of Echigo,” was a rival of Takeda Shingen. Despite their fierce enmity, a famous story tells of Kenshin sending salt to Shingen when his domain was cut off by a salt embargo. Kenshin believed that wars should be fought with swords, not starvation. This honorable gesture made him a symbol of bushidō—the way of the warrior—and earned him admiration even from his enemies.
